          <p>When the Sun rises, the mountain slopes heat up faster than the valley floor. The air touching the warm rock heats up too — and warm air, as we know, <strong>rises</strong>.</p>

          <p>As that warm air floats upward, cooler air from down in the valley rushes in to take its place. This gentle upslope breeze is called a <strong>valley breeze</strong>.</p>

          <p>You'll often see cumulus clouds forming over the peaks by afternoon — that's the rising air condensing.</p>

        </div>

      </div>

        <div class="chapter-tag reveal">SCENE 03 · NIGHTTIME</div>

        <h2 class="h-title reveal">The mountain<br><em>breeze</em> downhill.</h2>

        <p class="sub reveal">Cool slopes, dense air, a quiet descent.</p>

        <div class="body-text reveal">

          <p>After sunset, the mountain slopes lose heat quickly. The air right above them cools down, becomes <strong>denser and heavier</strong>, and starts to sink.</p>

          <p>Gravity pulls this cold air down the slopes into the valley below — a flow we call the <strong>mountain breeze</strong>.</p>

          <p>Valleys can get surprisingly chilly at night because of this draining cold air. Farmers care about this a lot!</p>

        </div>

      </div>

        <div class="chapter-tag reveal">13.2.2 — PLANETARY WINDS</div>

        <h2 class="h-title reveal">From local<br>to <em>global</em>.</h2>

        <p class="sub reveal">The whole planet has its own wind system.</p>

        <div class="body-text reveal">

          <p>Local winds are small. But Earth itself is heated unevenly by the Sun — the equator gets a lot, the poles get very little. That big temperature difference sets up huge, permanent wind patterns called <strong>planetary winds</strong>.</p>

          <p>These winds would blow in straight lines… except the Earth <strong>spins</strong>. The rotation bends their path, curving them into the elegant loops you see on every world map.</p>

        <div class="chapter-tag reveal">13.2.3 — OCEAN CURRENTS</div>

        <h2 class="h-title reveal">The sea has<br><em>rivers</em> too.</h2>

        <p class="sub reveal">Giant loops of water, hidden in plain sight.</p>

        <div class="body-text reveal">

          <p>Oceans don't sit still. Wind drags the surface water along, while differences in <strong>temperature</strong> and <strong>saltiness</strong> set the whole ocean moving in enormous circular flows called <strong>gyres</strong>.</p>

          <p>These currents carry warm water from the equator toward the poles, and cold water back again. They're Earth's <strong>climate engine</strong> — without them, the tropics would boil and the poles would freeze.</p>

          <p>The famous <strong>Gulf Stream</strong>, for example, makes Northern Europe much warmer than it should be at that latitude.</p>

    }
