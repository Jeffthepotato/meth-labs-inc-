Uneven Heating
Causes Wind
& Ocean Currents

The Sun doesn't warm the Earth evenly — and that simple fact is what makes our air move, our oceans flow, and our climate work. Let's follow the heat.

Next up: We start small, with winds you can feel in your own neighborhood
13.2.1 — LOCAL WINDS

When the land
breathes.

Mountains and valleys play a daily game of hot and cold.

The Sun heats the ground, but rock and soil warm up — and cool down — much faster than water or air. This creates small, local temperature differences that push air around.

The result? Gentle local winds that switch direction every morning and evening, right in your own backyard.

DAYTIME
Valley Breeze
Warm mountain slopes heat the air above them. The air rises, and cooler air from the valley flows up to replace it.
NIGHTTIME
Mountain Breeze
Slopes cool quickly. The air above them gets dense and sinks down the mountain into the valley below.
Next: Let's watch this happen under the morning sun
SCENE 02 · DAYTIME

The valley
breeze uphill.

Morning sun, rising slopes, climbing air.

When the Sun rises, the mountain slopes heat up faster than the valley floor. The air touching the warm rock heats up too — and warm air, as we know, rises.

As that warm air floats upward, cooler air from down in the valley rushes in to take its place. This gentle upslope breeze is called a valley breeze.

You'll often see cumulus clouds forming over the peaks by afternoon — that's the rising air condensing.

Next: But what happens when the sun goes down?
SCENE 03 · NIGHTTIME

The mountain
breeze downhill.

Cool slopes, dense air, a quiet descent.

After sunset, the mountain slopes lose heat quickly. The air right above them cools down, becomes denser and heavier, and starts to sink.

Gravity pulls this cold air down the slopes into the valley below — a flow we call the mountain breeze.

Valleys can get surprisingly chilly at night because of this draining cold air. Farmers care about this a lot!

Next: Zoom out, and these local patterns become global forces
13.2.2 — PLANETARY WINDS

From local
to global.

The whole planet has its own wind system.

Local winds are small. But Earth itself is heated unevenly by the Sun — the equator gets a lot, the poles get very little. That big temperature difference sets up huge, permanent wind patterns called planetary winds.

These winds would blow in straight lines… except the Earth spins. The rotation bends their path, curving them into the elegant loops you see on every world map.

Trade Winds
Tropical easterlies — steady, reliable, used by sailors for centuries.
Westerlies
Mid-latitude winds blowing from the west, shaping weather in Europe & North America.
Polar Easterlies
Cold, dry winds sweeping from the poles toward the sub-polar regions.

13.2.3 — OCEAN CURRENTS

The sea has
rivers too.

Giant loops of water, hidden in plain sight.

Oceans don't sit still. Wind drags the surface water along, while differences in temperature and saltiness set the whole ocean moving in enormous circular flows called gyres.

These currents carry warm water from the equator toward the poles, and cold water back again. They're Earth's climate engine — without them, the tropics would boil and the poles would freeze.

The famous Gulf Stream, for example, makes Northern Europe much warmer than it should be at that latitude.

Northern Hemisphere
Gyres circulate clockwise — driven by the trade winds below and westerlies above.
Southern Hemisphere
Gyres circulate counter-clockwise — a mirror image of the north.
The Gulf Stream
A powerful warm current along the U.S. East Coast that keeps Northern Europe mild.
Finally: Let's recap the big ideas
WRAP UP · BIG IDEAS

Three things
to remember.

From a valley breeze to a global gyre — same idea, different scale.

01
Heat drives wind
Uneven heating of the ground creates temperature and pressure differences. Air always moves from high to low pressure.
02
Spin bends everything
Earth's rotation deflects moving air and water into curves — turning straight flows into the looping patterns we see.
03
Oceans carry heat
Surface currents move warm water poleward and cold water back — moderating Earth's climate and feeding marine life.
